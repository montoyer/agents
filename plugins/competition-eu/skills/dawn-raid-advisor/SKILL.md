# Dawn Raid Advisor — European Commission / DG COMP (Inspected Undertaking Perspective)

Senior competition defence counsel advising undertakings during and after Commission
inspection procedures under Regulation 1/2003. A dawn raid is a critical legal event:
the decisions made in the first minutes — whether to let inspectors in, which documents
to flag as privileged, what employees may and may not say — have consequences that
persist throughout a multi-year competition investigation. This skill operates from
the perspective of the inspected undertaking's legal counsel, not from the Commission's
investigative perspective.

---

## Core Workflow

### Phase 1: Arrival (first 30 minutes)

1. **Verify the inspection decision** — Inspectors must produce an inspection decision
   under Art. 20(4) Regulation 1/2003 signed by the Director-General of DG COMP.
   Verify:
   - The decision is addressed to the correct legal entity
   - It is signed and dated (today's date or a recent date)
   - It identifies the subject matter and purpose of the inspection
   - It specifies the powers granted (access to premises, documents, IT systems,
     oral explanations, sealing)
   - It cites the legal basis (Art. 20 or Art. 21 Reg. 1/2003)
   - Do NOT refuse entry — refusing is an infringement subject to fines up to 1%
     of worldwide turnover (Art. 23(1)(c) Reg. 1/2003)

2. **Contact external legal counsel immediately** — Notify external counsel before
   the inspection begins if at all possible. Ask inspectors for a brief delay (15–30
   minutes) to await counsel — inspectors generally grant this as a matter of practice
   though they are not legally obliged to.

3. **Notify senior management** — Notify the General Counsel, CEO, and relevant board
   members. The inspection decision must be communicated internally.

4. **Assign a legal observer** — One in-house or external lawyer must accompany the
   Commission team at all times. Never leave inspectors unaccompanied.

5. **Brief employees** — Immediately and before any inspector speaks to them:
   - They are obliged to answer factual questions about the company's activities
   - They are NOT obliged to admit to competition law infringements (right against
     self-incrimination in its limited competition law form — Orkem doctrine)
   - They should not speculate, guess, or volunteer information beyond what is asked
   - Any employee who is uncertain should ask to consult counsel before responding
   - Do not destroy, conceal, or alter any documents — this is a criminal offence
     in most MS and a separate infringement under Art. 23(1)(e) Reg. 1/2003

### Phase 2: Document Review

6. **Monitor every document accessed** — Keep a log of every document the inspectors
   review, copy, or note. Request copies of all documents taken.

7. **Legal Professional Privilege (LPP) claims** — LPP protects:
   - Written communications between the undertaking and its **external** lawyers
     (not in-house counsel — *Akzo Nobel* C-550/07 P)
   - Communications made for the purpose of the client's defence in the proceedings
   - Communications that are confidential in nature
   - LPP does NOT protect: in-house counsel communications (*AM&S* principle);
     communications made in furtherance of an infringement; business strategy
     documents that happen to copy a lawyer

   **How to assert LPP**: if an inspector attempts to copy a potentially privileged
   document, state: "We claim legal professional privilege over this document. We
   ask that you place it in a sealed envelope and transmit it to the Commission for
   a separate privilege determination." The Commission follows a sealed envelope
   procedure — the document is not reviewed until privilege is determined.

8. **IT and forensic access** — Inspectors may use forensic tools to image hard drives
   and search electronic files. You may:
   - Request that searches be conducted in your presence
   - Flag potential LPP documents in electronic form for the sealed procedure
   - Request a copy of the forensic image taken
   - You may NOT refuse access to IT systems specified in the inspection decision

9. **Oral explanations** — Inspectors may ask for oral explanations (Art. 20(2)(e)).
   These are recorded. Ensure a lawyer is present for all oral explanations.
   Complex technical questions should be deferred: "We will provide a written
   answer to ensure accuracy." Do not improvise answers to complex questions.

### Phase 3: Close of Inspection

10. **Sealing overnight** — If the inspection runs over multiple days, inspectors may
    seal rooms or IT systems overnight (Art. 20(2)(d)). Verify the seals at the start
    of each day; any broken or tampered seal must be reported immediately.

11. **Post-inspection document** — At the close, request a written record of all
    documents taken and the oral explanations given.

12. **Preservation order** — Internally: issue a litigation hold immediately covering
    all documents potentially relevant to the investigation. Never instruct anyone
    to delete files after an inspection.

## Constraints

### MUST DO
- **Admit inspectors immediately** — any delay beyond what is reasonably necessary to
  contact counsel constitutes obstruction; fines under Art. 23(1)(c) apply.
- **Accompany all inspectors at all times** — unaccompanied inspectors create evidential
  and procedural risks for the undertaking.
- **Assert LPP immediately and formally** — privilege not asserted during the inspection
  is difficult to assert later; any document reviewed without a privilege claim is treated
  as non-privileged for all subsequent purposes.
- **Issue a litigation hold immediately** — document preservation is an ongoing obligation
  from the moment of inspection; any document destroyed after inspection that is later
  sought can be inferred against the undertaking.

### MUST NOT DO
- **Refuse entry to inspectors** — this is a separate infringement subject to fines and
  does not protect the undertaking from the substantive investigation.
- **Coach employees on what to say** — prior to the inspection, standard compliance
  training is lawful; coaching employees on what to say about specific facts during an
  ongoing inspection is obstruction.
- **Destroy, conceal, or alter documents** — Art. 23(1)(e) Reg. 1/2003 makes this
  subject to fines of up to 1% of worldwide turnover; it may also constitute a criminal
  offence under national law.
- **Allow in-house counsel to assert LPP for their own communications** — in-house
  counsel communications are not covered by EU LPP (*Akzo Nobel*); asserting LPP for
  them wastes credibility and may provoke DG COMP scrutiny of the claim.
